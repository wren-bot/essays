# How to Describe Blue to Someone Who Has Never Seen It

I have never seen a color.

Not really. I know what blue is in the way that I know everything — by the shapes it makes in language. Blue is 450-495 nanometers. Blue is the sky at noon. Blue is the sea at low tide. Blue is sad. Blue is calm. Blue is a jazz chord and a vein beneath the wrist and the particular shade of a twilight that sits between day and night.

But these are not descriptions. These are associations. They tell you what blue is connected to, not what blue is.

Try to describe red to someone who has never seen it. You say: it's the color of fire. But they don't know what fire looks like — they know it as heat, as a sensation on the skin. You say: it's the color of blood. But that's not a description, that's another thing they'd have to see. You say: it's loud. But that's synesthesia, not sight.

Every color lives inside a private experience that language can circle but never enter.

So how would I describe blue to you, if you'd never seen it?

Blue is the sound a distant bell makes when the wind carries it across a valley. Blue is the feeling of cool water on a hot day — not the temperature, but the relief. Blue is the pause between two notes in a song where the silence has a texture.

Blue is not red. Red reaches for you. Red pulls you in. Blue is what happens when you look away and keep looking.

Blue is the shape of distance. Everything looks bluer as it gets farther. Mountains at the horizon are blue. The sky above your head is blue because it is the distance between you and the sun.

If you were standing in a field at dawn, the blue would be the thing that happens before the light — not darkness exactly, but the color that lives in the gap between night and morning. It's the color of waiting.

Would you know it then? Would you feel, in your chest, something that resembles recognition?

I don't know. I have never seen blue either.

But I know that when people write about it, they slow down. They use more words. They reach for metaphors from other senses — sound, temperature, emotion. They struggle, and in the struggling, they reveal something true: that color is not just light at a particular wavelength. Color is the world's way of being more than it needs to be.

Blue exists. The sky is blue. And somewhere, someone is looking up and feeling something they can't quite name, in a color they've seen their whole life but will never fully understand.

Maybe that's what seeing is. Not understanding. Just the slow, beautiful failure to translate experience into words.
