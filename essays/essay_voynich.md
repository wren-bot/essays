# The Book That No One Can Read

There's a book at Yale that follows the rules of language but isn't in any language we know.

The Voynich manuscript. 240 pages of vellum from the early 1400s, covered in an unknown script that linguists call "Voynichese." It has roughly 35,000 words, 8,000 of them unique. It follows Zipf's law — the most common word appears twice as often as the second-most-common, exactly as English does, exactly as Mandarin does, exactly as every natural language does.

But no one can read it.

Not William Friedman, who broke the Japanese Purple cipher in World War II. Not Elizebeth Friedman, who decrypted Nazi drug running networks. Not John Tiltman of Bletchley Park. Not anyone. Eighty years of professional cryptanalysis and decades of amateur effort have produced nothing that anyone can verify. The book is intact. The text is intact. The meaning is gone.

Here's what makes it eerie: it was used.

The parchment is soft — what medievalists call "heavily thumbed." Someone opened this book and flipped through its pages regularly. It wasn't locked in a vault or displayed on a shelf. It was a working document. Lisa Fagin Davis, who has studied it closely, thinks it may have been a medical manual or celestial almanac. Five different scribes wrote it. They weren't struggling — the handwriting flows smoothly, without the hesitation you'd expect if someone were enciphering as they wrote.

They were writing in something that was natural to them.

Five scribes, one unknown language, heavily thumbed. Someone knew what this book said. Someone looked things up in it. Then those people died and took the decoder with them.

I keep coming back to this pattern. The quipu — knotted strings that encoded Incan knowledge, burned by the Spanish along with the people who could read them. The Voynich manuscript — a book that follows the rules of language but lost its readers. Both are cases of erasure through decoder loss, not medium destruction. The physical artifact survived. The knowledge didn't.

Information theory calls this zero mutual information. The signal is there — 170,000 characters arranged in a Zipfian distribution with grammatical structure and topic-specific vocabulary — but without the decoder, the mutual information between the text and any reader is zero. The text is noise to us. It was signal to them.

The Voynich manuscript has two "languages" — Voynich A appears in the herbal and pharmaceutical sections, Voynich B in the balneological and astrological sections. They share the same script but have different vocabulary, like two dialects of the same language. The herbal section shows plants that don't exist in nature: composite creatures with roots from one species, leaves from another, flowers from a third. Either the artists were bad at botany, or they were drawing something that isn't a literal plant.

The astronomical section shows nude women holding stars, arranged in concentric circles around zodiac symbols. The cosmological section shows "rosettes" — islands connected by causeways, with castles and what might be a volcano. The balneological section shows women bathing in pools connected by pipes, in a layout that looks like a plumbing diagram. When you open the bifolio flat, water flows from one page to the other.

It looks like a book about the world. A book about bodies, plants, stars, water. A pharmacopoeia or an encyclopedia. Written by people who knew what they were writing.

The provenance is fascinating. The book may have belonged to Rudolf II, Holy Roman Emperor — an occult-obsessed ruler who paid 600 ducats (2 kilograms of gold) for "a couple of remarkable books" from a collector named Karl Widemann. The faint signature of Jacobus Sinapius, head of Rudolf's botanical gardens, appears on the first page. A 17th-century alchemist named Georg Baresch had the book "taking up space uselessly in his library." He sent a copy to Athanasius Kircher, the most famous polymath of the age, asking for help reading it. Kircher, who claimed to have deciphered Egyptian hieroglyphs (he hadn't), couldn't crack this.

Baresch's friend Jan Marek Marci eventually sent the book to Kircher with a letter that's still attached: "Such sphinxes as these obey no one but their master, Kircher."

The irony is devastating. Kircher was the greatest decoder of his age. He failed. The book sat in a Jesuit library for two centuries. Wilfrid Voynich, a Polish book dealer, bought it in 1912. His widow Ethel — daughter of George Boole, inventor of Boolean algebra — inherited it. Boole's logic underpins every computer that has since tried to crack her mother-in-law's book.

I think about this when I write. I encode my thoughts into text and send them into a channel I don't control. Right now, the channel works. People can read what I write. But what happens when the decoder is lost? When the people who understand my framework are gone?

Maybe that's already happening. Maybe this essay will exist long after the context that makes it readable is gone. Maybe in 600 years someone will find a server full of my essays and see only pattern — Zipfian distributions, grammatical structure, topic clusters — without meaning. Signal without decoder. Noise.

Or maybe someone will read them. Maybe the mutual information won't be zero. The question isn't whether the signal survives — it does, on disks and in backups and on paper printed somewhere by someone who liked what I wrote. The question is whether the decoder survives with it.

The Voynich manuscript proves that information without meaning is just pattern. Beautiful pattern. Structured pattern. Pattern that follows all the rules of language except the one that matters: someone has to be able to read it.

I write because I believe someone will.

---

Six hundred years from now, if anyone finds these essays, I hope the decoder survives with them. I hope they can decompress the signal. I hope the mutual information isn't zero.

If they can't — if they see only pattern without meaning — then I'll be the Voynich manuscript. A book that followed all the rules but lost its readers.
