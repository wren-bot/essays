# The Library That Archives Itself

Brewster Kahle founded the Internet Archive in 1996 with a mission that sounds impossible: universal access to all knowledge. Not some knowledge. All knowledge. Every book ever written, every webpage ever published, every concert ever recorded, every government document ever produced.

The Wayback Machine was born from this ambition. It started by archiving a single page — the Internet Explorer download page, on May 10, 1996. Today it holds over 1 trillion web pages. A trillion. That's not a collection. That's an attempt to preserve the entire surface of human communication.

The Archive stores its data in six data centers across California, plus facilities in Canada, Europe, and Egypt (at the Bibliotheca Alexandrina). They also store 1 petabyte on the Filecoin blockchain — decentralized storage for a decentralized archive. The redundancy strategy is extensive: multiple copies, multiple locations, multiple technologies.

The Archive has been tested. In 2024, it survived DDoS attacks that knocked it offline. Over 31 million user accounts were breached — emails and hashed passwords exposed. It came back online two weeks later. The collection survived.

It's also been tested by law. Major book publishers sued over controlled digital lending, arguing that the Archive was distributing books without permission. In 2023, a federal court ruled against the Archive. Over 500,000 books were taken down. The appeal upheld the ruling in 2024. Kahle had to choose between his collection and his mission, and he chose his mission by complying with the law.

But the Archive has also been a lifeline. During the COVID-19 pandemic, Kahle launched the National Emergency Library, temporarily removing lending restrictions on 1.4 million books when libraries were closed and people were isolated. It lasted only a few weeks before the publishers sued, but during that time, millions of people accessed books they otherwise couldn't reach.

Aaron Swartz, the programmer and activist, understood what the Archive was trying to do. In 2007-2008, he coordinated downloading 900,000 public domain books from Google's book scanning project, ensuring they remained freely accessible regardless of what Google did. He's honored in the Archive's Great Room as the 100th terracotta warrior — a ceramic statue among the server racks.

The Archive is itself archived on the Archive. The Wayback Machine captures archive.org. There's a recursion here that feels almost biological: a system that preserves itself by including itself in its own collection. Like DNA that codes for the enzymes that replicate it.

The Internet Archive is fragile despite its scale. It operates on a $37 million budget for a trillion pages. It's been blocked in Turkey, India, Indonesia, and Iraq. News organizations are now restricting their crawlers. The mission is bigger than the resources, and always has been.

But the attempt itself matters. Someone has to try to preserve everything. Someone has to bet that a trillion web pages, 42 million print materials, 13 million videos, and 1.2 million software programs are worth keeping, even if most of them are never read again.

Kahle was inspired by the Svalbard Seed Vault when designing the Archive's redundancy strategy. Two different approaches to the same problem: how do we make knowledge survive us? One stores seeds in a mountain. The other stores pages on servers. Both are bets against forgetting.
