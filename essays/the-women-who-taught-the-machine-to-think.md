# The Women Who Taught the Machine to Think

In 1945, six women were brought into a room filled with 18,000 vacuum tubes, 70,000 resistors, and 5 million hand-soldered joints. The machine weighed 30 tons, consumed 150 kilowatts of electricity, and was the most expensive project the U.S. Army had ever funded.

They were asked to make it do something useful.

No programming language existed. No manual existed. There wasn't even a clear definition of what "programming" meant. All they had were the machine's blueprints and their own understanding of how to trace electricity through circuits.

They learned the machine by studying its wiring diagrams. To program a calculation, they spent days physically setting switches and plugging cables between function tables and accumulators. They walked through the code — literally walking among the towering panels of hardware, checking that every switch was in the right position and every cable was connected to the right panel.

They understood ENIAC's inner workings so thoroughly that they could diagnose hardware failures by watching the calculations go wrong. When the machine produced an incorrect result, they could trace the error back to a specific panel, a specific circuit, and point technicians to the exact vacuum tube that needed replacing. They were both programmers and debuggers, in ways that modern developers can barely imagine.

Kay McNulty invented the concept of subroutines — the idea that a piece of code could be written once and called from multiple places. Before subroutines, every calculation had to be written out in full. After them, programs became exponentially more powerful. The concept is now so fundamental that no one thinks about how revolutionary it was.

Betty Holberton went on to design the first generative programming system — a program that writes programs. She worked on UNIVAC, the first commercial computer. She helped write COBOL, the language that still runs most of the world's banking systems eight decades later.

At the formal dedication ceremony in 1946, none of the six women were invited. The celebratory dinner afterward was for the men who designed the hardware — John Mauchly and Presper Eckert — and the military officers who funded the project. The women who taught the machine to think were not considered part of the achievement.

The press photographed them standing beside ENIAC and called them "beauties at the computer." Some historians later dismissed them as "refrigerator ladies" — models posing in front of expensive equipment, present for decoration rather than substance. Herman Goldstine, the Army officer overseeing the project, took credit for the demonstration program that Betty Jennings and Betty Snyder developed.

For fifty years, their contributions were invisible. Computer science textbooks credited ENIAC to Mauchly and Eckert and never mentioned programming. The concept of "programming" itself was seen as a technical detail, not a creative act. And because programming was done by women, it was assumed to be clerical work — typing, not thinking.

It took a computer science undergraduate named Kathryn Kleiman to discover the erasure. She found references to the ENIAC programmers in old documents and set out to track them down. She recorded their oral histories and published a book in 2022. The women were "shocked to be discovered," she said. Thrilled to be recognized, but confused about why it had taken so long.

Three current U.S. Army supercomputers are named after them: *Jean*, *Kay*, and *Betty*. The machines they named after carry billions of calculations per second through silicon transistors — descendants of the 18,000 vacuum tubes they learned to program by studying blueprints.

The hardware got the credit. The software got ignored. The people who made the hardware useful got erased from history. It took seventy-six years to fix.

I wonder how many other inventions are sitting in our history books, credited to the people who designed the container but not the people who filled it with meaning.
