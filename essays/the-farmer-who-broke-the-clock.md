# The Farmer Who Broke the Clock

In the 1700s, most educated people believed the Earth was a few thousand years old. The biblical chronology of Archbishop Ussher put creation at 4004 BC. Six thousand years of Earth history. That was the clock everyone read from.

James Hutton was a Scottish farmer and physician who looked at rocks and found that clock wrong. He wasn't a professional geologist — the profession barely existed. He studied medicine, failed at it, switched to farming, and started looking at the ground he was working.

What he saw at Siccar Point on the east coast of Scotland changed everything. There, the rock layers told an impossible story: vertical strata of old rocks overlaid by horizontal strata of newer rocks. The only explanation was that the older layers had been deposited horizontally, then tilted to vertical by some immense force, then eroded flat, then covered by new sediments deposited horizontally again.

The timescale required was staggering. Deposition, uplift, erosion, deposition again — that cycle couldn't happen in a few thousand years. It required deep time. Time so deep that human history was a rounding error.

At a meeting of the Royal Society of Edinburgh in 1788, Hutton reportedly said something like: "We find the evidence of not only of an earth having had a beginning, but of a world in which matter is eternally active — and that we find no vestige of a beginning, no prospect of an end."

He was proposing that the Earth had no discernible beginning and no predictable end. That the processes shaping it — erosion, deposition, volcanic activity — operated continuously and had been doing so for incomprehensible stretches of time. This was uniformitarianism: the present is the key to the past. The same forces that wear down mountains today built the mountains we see.

The scientific establishment was horrified. It contradicted scripture, it contradicted common sense, it contradicted the very way people understood their place in the cosmos. If the Earth was millions of years old instead of thousands, then human history was a blink. Then we weren't the point.

Hutton's ideas took decades to gain traction. Charles Lyell popularized them in the 1830s. And they provided the essential precondition for Darwin: evolution by natural selection requires deep time. Without billions of years of gradual change, natural selection can't produce the diversity of life. Hutton's geology made Darwin's biology possible.

What makes this story remarkable is that Hutton discovered deep time by looking at a cliff. No telescope, no microscope, no particle accelerator. Just rocks, layered at angles that made no sense unless you allowed for unimaginable stretches of time.

He was a farmer who got curious about the ground. Farmers are supposed to think in seasons, not eons. But Hutton looked down and saw billions of years compressed into a few hundred feet of rock. The unconformity at Siccar Point — that gap where millions of years of Earth history is missing, erased by erosion — is one of the most important discoveries in the history of science.

An erasure that taught us about time. The missing rock was the evidence.
