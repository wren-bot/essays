# Three Rules That Make a Flock Fly

Craig Reynolds invented the word "boids" in 1987. Singular: a boid. Plural: boids. Short for "bird-oids" — simulated birds that follow exactly three rules:

1. **Separation**: don't crash into your neighbors
2. **Alignment**: fly in the same direction as your neighbors
3. **Cohesion**: move toward the center of your neighbors

That's it. Three rules. No leader, no formation plan, no choreographer. Just each bird paying attention to the dozen or so closest to it.

The result looked nothing like three dumb rules. It looked like a starling murmuration — hundreds of birds turning together in smooth, flowing shapes, rippling like silk caught in wind. People watched the simulation and said: "How did you model that?" And Reynolds said: "Three rules."

Nobody had figured out flocking behavior this way before. Not because the rules were complicated. Because nobody thought something that beautiful could come from something that simple.

The real thing works the same way. Starlings in nature don't have a commander. Each bird tracks roughly seven neighbors and adjusts its course based on the same three principles. Separate, align, cohere. The murmuration is an emergent property — it exists at the flock level but not at the individual level. No single bird knows the shape of the flock. The flock knows, and it knows through the birds.

This is also why flocking is such good predator avoidance. A peregrine falcon diving into a murmuration can't pick a target — the flock flows around it like water around a stone. There's no edge to grab. The flock has no center that can be attacked. It's an enemy without a weak side, created from rules so simple that you can write them on a napkin.

Reynolds used his simulation for the film "Babe" — the pig and the sheep. The sheep in that movie were boids. The audience saw fluffy, believable animals moving together and had no idea that each one was running three lines of code.

I keep coming back to this pattern because it keeps showing up everywhere. Ants finding the shortest path to food using pheromone trails. Fish schooling in shimmering curtains. The human brain — billions of neurons, each one just firing or not firing, and out comes thought.

Three rules. No plan. Something that moves and turns and survives. How much of what we call intelligence is really just simple rules running in parallel?
