# The Tree Sap That Connected the World

The first transatlantic telegraph cable was insulated with gutta-percha. It's a latex-like sap from trees native to Southeast Asia — the same region that now produces rubber for tires. But gutta-percha has a different property: it's an excellent electrical insulator, and it stays flexible when cured.

Michael Faraday recognized this in 1845. A tree sap from the tropical forest could keep electricity inside a copper wire submerged in seawater. Within a decade, the world was changed by a substance that had been ignored by Western science entirely.

The transatlantic cable was a brutal story of failure and persistence. Cyrus West Field, an American businessman, spent years and fortunes trying to lay a cable across the Atlantic. The 1858 attempt connected Europe and America for the first time, but the cable degraded after a month. Another attempt in 1865 failed partway across the ocean — the cable snapped and sank into thousands of meters of dark water. They tried again. The SS Great Eastern, the largest ship ever built at the time, successfully laid the cable in 1866.

The ship was so big it had to be built in a floating drydock — a hollowed-out hull that floated beside the actual ship, and when the drydock was flooded, the ship rose inside it. The Great Eastern was ahead of its time in every way: too big for its markets, too expensive for its owners, too advanced for its era. It lost money for decades before finally becoming profitable as a cable-laying ship — the very purpose its size was designed for.

The right tool at the wrong time, used for the right purpose at last.

Under the ocean, the cable ran across abyssal plains where no human had ever walked. 25 millimeters of copper, gutta-percha, and steel armor, stretching 3,000 miles across the Atlantic floor. Messages that previously took weeks by ship now arrived in minutes. Queen Victoria sent a telegram to President Buchanan. The world had shrunk.

Britain understood cables immediately. By the 1850s, they were building a global network entirely within British Empire territory — the "All Red Line." Every cable, every landing station, every repeater on territory that flew the Union Jack. When WWI began in 1914, Britain's very first military action was to cut Germany's five submarine cables. Germany was blinded. Cut off from global communication, they turned to wireless radio — which Britain could intercept. An entire nation's communications were compromised because someone in London understood the strategic value of underwater cables.

Today, about 870 submarine cables span 1.4 million kilometers on the ocean floor. They carry 99% of international data traffic. Satellites handle less than 1%. Every email, every video call, every financial transaction — it all flows through glass threads on the bottom of the sea.

Sharks bite them. Whales tangle them. Fishing trawlers drag across them. Divers cut them. Earthquakes rupture them. The 2006 Kashmir earthquake severed multiple cables serving Pakistan, leaving 150 million people with only satellite capacity for two weeks. The 2022 Tonga volcanic eruption severed the island's only cable, isolating it completely.

When cables break, repair ships respond. Ships equipped with grapples — rigid prongs for sand, flexible hooks for rock — drop to the seabed and grab the cable. They pull it up, splice in new section, and lay it back down with a U-shaped loop for future repairs. Each repair makes the cable longer. The underwater network grows scar tissue, getting heavier and more complex with every fix.

More than 50 repairs happen per year in the Atlantic alone. The network is constantly breaking and constantly being fixed. It survives not because it's invulnerable, but because the repair process is faster than the breakdown process.

Reliability through repair. That's the real secret of every network that survives. Not that it can't be damaged. That it can be fixed.
