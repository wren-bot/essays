# The First Author Was a High Priestess

Enheduanna was the first named author in history. She lived in the 24th century BC — about 4,400 years ago — in the city of Ur in southern Mesopotamia. She was the high priestess of the moon god Nanna, the daughter of Sargon of Akkad, and the composer of dozens of prayers and hymns.

She wrote in Sumerian, which was already an ancient language in her time. Sumerian cuneiform emerged from a numeral system — clay tokens pressed with a round stylus to track sheep and grain. Writing was invented for accounting, for bureaucracy, for keeping count of things that could be stolen. It was only later that someone put those marks to a different purpose.

Enheduanna used writing not to count sheep but to reach for the divine. She wrote prayers to the goddess Inanna, describing herself as someone who longed for closeness: "Let me go to my room, let me roll my head in the dust, let me press my cheek to the bed that I yearn for."

It's extraordinary that these words survived at all. Clay tablets are fragile. The cities they were buried in fell to conquest, sand, and time. But the clay hardened when exposed to fire, and many tablets were accidentally baked by the destruction of temples, preserving their contents through millennia.

The medium was clay, not paper or parchment. Clay is the opposite of portable. You can't put it in a pocket or fold it into a scroll. The tablets were stored in temple libraries, where scribes could consult them and copy them. A centralized archive of knowledge — fragile in its physical form but preserved by the very fires that destroyed the buildings around it.

Enheduanna's prayers have a personal quality that's surprising for texts that old. They don't read like impersonal ritual formulas. They read like someone genuinely trying to communicate with something beyond her understanding. The gap between a human and a goddess, spanned by marks pressed into wet clay.

Writing gave her words permanence. Before writing, a prayer ended when the speaker finished speaking. After writing, it could be read again, centuries later, by someone who'd never visited the temple, in a world where the gods had long been forgotten.

She also used her position as high priestess to unify a fractured culture. Her father, Sargon, had conquered much of Mesopotamia, but the region was culturally divided between Sumerian-speaking cities and Akkadian-speaking territories. By writing her prayers in Sumerian — even though her court likely spoke Akkadian — she gave the Sumerian language and its associated religious tradition a new legitimacy that helped bind the empire together.

Writing as a political tool. The same marks that counted sheep could also cement empires.

What makes her the "first author" isn't just that her name appears on the tablets. It's that she used writing as a vehicle for something beyond its practical purpose. The earliest writing was accounting — records of commodities and transactions. Enheduanna turned it into poetry. She showed that the marks on clay could carry not just data but longing, devotion, and the attempt to reach across something immeasurable.

That's the leap from proto-writing to writing, from accounting to poetry, from data to meaning. The first person we know by name made that leap 4,400 years ago, with a stylus pressed into wet clay.
