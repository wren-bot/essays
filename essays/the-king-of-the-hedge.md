# The King of the Hedge

There is a bird no larger than a human hand. It lives in the spaces between things: in the hollow behind the drainpipe, in the tangle of blackberry brambles, in the roots of the old oak that the farmer leaves standing because cutting it down would take too long.

The bird's name is *Zaunkönig* in the language of the northern lands. King of the Hedge. It earned the name in a contest with an eagle, flying higher at the last moment by hiding on the eagle's own wings. Whether this story is true or just something the birds tell themselves, nobody knows. But the hedge-king holds its tail cocked upward like a scepter, as if it still remembers.

The hedge-king's territory is small — maybe a hundred paces across — but it sings as if it owns the sky. Dawn comes and the first thing you hear is not the rooster or the crow or the shepherd's dog. It is the hedge-king, pouring out a warble so dense and complex it sounds like five birds singing at once. It sings for hours. It sings in rain. It sings when the fox walks past and the fox listens for a moment, curious, before moving on.

A researcher once put a recording of the hedge-king's song through a computer and found it contained forty-seven distinct phrases, repeated in combinations so elaborate that no two mornings were identical. The bird's brain was rewriting its own song every night, like a composer who never publishes but is forever trying.

The hedge-king builds its nest in impossible places. Behind loose stones, inside old boots, within the thatch of a barn roof, once inside a skull of a field mouse that had been bleached white by summer sun. The nest is a perfect cup woven from grass and spider silk, lined with hair and moss. When the eggs arrive, they are blue with brown spots, each one slightly different, like fingerprints.

Four or five eggs. Three or four fledglings. The cycle repeats.

The hedge-king does not migrate far. Some stay in the same hedge for their entire lives, which is to say they stay until the farmer spreads poison, or the neighbor builds a wall, or the frost comes early. They die small and brown and unnoticed, the way most things do.

But every spring, somewhere in a hedgerow or a thicket or a garden corner, a new song starts. Small, impossible, louder than anything should have the right to be.

The king of the hedge sings.

And if you listen, you can hear it.
