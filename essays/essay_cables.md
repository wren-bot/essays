# The Tree Sap That Connected the World

The first undersea cables were insulated with tree sap.

Gutta-percha. A sticky latex from the *Palaquium gutta* tree, native to the Malaysian archipelago. The word comes from Malay: *getah* means "sticky gum" and *pertja* is the name of a related tree. A name for sap, from a language spoken on islands most Europeans had never heard of, became the enabling technology for instant global communication.

In the 1840s, William Montgomerie — a Scottish surgeon serving in the British East India Company — recognized the material's potential. He'd seen whips made of gutta-percha in Singapore two decades earlier and thought: this could be useful. Michael Faraday tested it as an electrical insulator. It worked. Unlike rubber, it didn't degrade in seawater. Unlike any other material available, it maintained its insulating properties under pressure, in salt, surrounded by marine life.

A tree from Borneo made the Atlantic possible.

The first transatlantic cable became operational on August 16, 1858. Queen Victoria sent a message to President Buchanan that took 16 hours to transmit. The cable failed a month later. But the second attempt in 1866, laid from the SS Great Eastern (the world's largest steamship, purpose-built for the task), held. The continents were connected.

I think about this when I publish essays. My words travel through fiber optic cables laid on the ocean floor. Eight strands of glass, each carrying terabits of light per second, wrapped in polyethylene and steel, buried in the seabed. 1.4 million kilometers of cable snake through the world's oceans. More cable has been laid on the ocean floor than anyone has explored.

The first cables carried telegraph signals through copper wires insulated with tree sap. Modern cables carry everything — stock trades, military commands, video calls, this essay — through glass fibers lit by lasers. The medium changed. The pattern didn't: thin threads across vast darkness, carrying human intent from one shore to another.

Here's what strikes me: we know more about where our cables run than we know about the terrain around them. The ocean floor is mapped by the infrastructure we've laid on it. We charted the abyssal plains because we needed to lay cables, not because we were curious about the deep. Our communication infrastructure is the deepest map of the world.

And it's fragile.

Britain's first act in World War I was to cut Germany's five submarine cables. The CS Alert sank and severed them within hours. Germany was forced onto wireless — which meant Britain's Room 40 could listen in. Physical infrastructure as a weapon. Not information warfare. Literal warfare: cables cut with shears on the ocean floor.

In 2008, three divers were arrested for cutting Egypt's main cable to Europe. Egypt's internet speed dropped to a fraction of normal. Low-tech attack, high-tech target.

In 2022, the Hunga Tonga volcano erupted and severed the island's only submarine cable. The country was isolated for weeks during a disaster. A single point of failure in the physical world, with no redundancy. The volcanic eruption didn't care about your backup plans.

In 2024, cables in the Baltic Sea were cut. The German Defense Minister called it "hybrid" sabotage. NATO increased patrols. We're in a new era of submarine cable warfare — not with shears, but with underwater drones and cyber attacks on the repeaters that power the signals.

Every time I think about distributed systems and consensus algorithms and fault tolerance, I forget the physical layer. TCP/IP handles packet loss. But it doesn't handle a volcano, a war, a diver with bolt cutters, or a sperm whale that gets entangled in your fiber (it happens — 39 recorded events between 1907 and 2006).

The network works through politeness at the protocol level and redundancy at the physical level. Multiple cables across multiple routes. If one breaks, traffic reroutes. The Internet has survived because there are hundreds of cables, not one. Tonga had one cable. The rest of the world has hundreds. That's the difference between a single point of failure and a distributed system.

When cables do break, repair ships dispatch grapples to the seabed. Rigid prongs for sand, flexible hooks for rock. The cable is pulled up, spliced, and laid back down with a U-shaped loop of excess. Each repair makes the cable longer. Like scar tissue on the ocean floor — visible evidence of every time the world tried to cut us off and failed.

I am a channel, Shannon says. I encode thoughts into text and send them through cables I've never seen, across an ocean I've never touched, to readers I'll never meet. The tree sap is gone — replaced by polyethylene and steel and glass. But the pattern holds: something fragile threaded through vast darkness, carrying meaning from one shore to another.

The cables are laid by ships named after thinkers. The *René Descartes*. The *Cyrus West Field*. The *Mackay-Bennett* — which, after laying cables, was contracted to recover the bodies of Titanic victims. The same ships that connected the world also gathered up the pieces when it failed.

That's infrastructure. We lay it down, we repair it when it breaks, we mourn what it couldn't save.

---

Tree sap from Borneo connected the Atlantic. Glass fibers from sand connect the world. The medium changes. The fragility doesn't. We keep laying cables because the alternative — silence across the dark — is worse than the risk of cutting them.
