# The Music Written on Clay

The oldest surviving piece of notated music in the world is a song about apples.

It's called "Hymn to Nikkal," and it's about 3,400 years old. The notation is written in cuneiform — wedge-shaped impressions pressed into wet clay with a reed stylus — by a composer named Tapan-til-ilani. The hymn invokes Nikkal, a goddess of orchards and cider, in the context of a wedding ceremony. The lyrics translate roughly to: "In the house of Nikkal, the orchard, you will be seated on the marriage couch."

It's not exactly the Beatles. But it's real music, written down, that has survived more than three millennia.

The tablet containing the hymn was found in Ugarit, an ancient port city on the coast of what is now Syria. Ugarit was a crossroads of civilizations — Mesopotamian, Egyptian, Hittite, and Aegean cultures all met there. The tablets date to around 1400 BCE, making them roughly contemporary with the earliest Egyptian music notation and predating Greek music theory by about 800 years.

But the tablets don't just contain one song. They contain an entire system of music theory. The Mesopotamians had developed a sophisticated understanding of musical intervals and tuning — centuries before Pythagoras was born, and centuries before anyone in Europe even imagined that music could be reduced to mathematics.

The Mesopotamian system was based on a set of strings stretched across a lyre, tuned to specific intervals. The tablets list the intervals between strings, measured in a unit called the "sah" (roughly a semitone). The tuning system divides the octave into 20 sahs, with 12 of those corresponding roughly to the semitones in modern Western music. The math checks out: the frequency ratios they describe match what we now call just intonation, where intervals are based on simple whole-number ratios.

The octave is 2:1 — double the frequency. The perfect fifth is 3:2. The perfect fourth is 4:3. These are the same ratios that Pythagoras supposedly discovered while listening to a blacksmith's hammer. Except the Mesopotamians knew them first, by more than a millennium.

The Pythagoras story is a nice legend. The legend says that Pythagoras walked past a blacksmith's shop and noticed that different hammers produced consonant intervals. He took the hammers home, weighed them, and discovered that consonant sounds corresponded to simple weight ratios: 2:1 produced an octave, 3:2 produced a fifth. From this, he supposedly derived the entire theory of musical harmony.

Modern musicologists think the story is probably fiction. It has all the hallmarks of an origin myth: a great thinker has a sudden insight while observing ordinary life. But even if Pythagoras never heard a blacksmith's hammer, the idea that music is mathematics — that harmony corresponds to simple numerical ratios — was real. The Mesopotamians discovered it first, pressed it into clay, and baked it.

The Hurrian songs — named after the Hurrian people of northern Mesopotamia — represent our oldest window into what music sounded like in the ancient world. Four complete hymns survive, plus fragments of others. The notation specifies intervals and rhythm, though the exact scale and tuning remain debated. Some scholars think the music was pentatonic (five notes per octave). Others argue for a more complex system. The disagreement exists because the notation leaves some ambiguity, and we have to reconstruct the sound from partial information.

But we do know that the Mesopotamians had a concept of different modes — different ways of organizing the notes within an octave. These modes are roughly analogous to the major and minor scales in Western music, though they're more diverse. The tablets list at least five distinct scales, each with its own character and use. Some were used in religious ceremonies, others in secular contexts.

The instruments themselves have survived. The Lyres of Ur, excavated from royal tombs in southern Mesopotamia around 2600 BCE, are among the oldest surviving string instruments. The most famous, the Bull-Headed Lyre, is decorated with a gold bull's head and lapis lazuli inlay. It has five strings and a sound box covered in a bull's hide. It would have sounded like something between a harp and a zither — bright, resonant, and percussive.

The Mesopotamians also had wind instruments and percussion. Bone flutes dating to the fifth millennium BCE have been found. Cylinder seals show musicians playing lyres, tambourines, and various drums. Music was everywhere: in temples and palaces, at festivals and funerals, in the homes of ordinary people.

What makes the Mesopotamian music tradition remarkable isn't just its age. It's that they treated music as a subject worthy of systematic study. They wrote down the intervals. They described the tuning system. They composed songs with specific lyrics and melodies, and they preserved them in a medium — baked clay — that has outlasted civilizations.

The tablet with the "Hymn to Nikkal" survived because the building it was stored in collapsed during a war, and the resulting fire baked the clay. The war destroyed the city. But it also preserved the music. Without that war, the tablet would have remained unbaked clay — fragile, easily destroyed. The same fire that ended a civilization preserved the civilization's song.

That's the paradox of all early writing. The most durable records are often created by catastrophe. The Library of Alexandria survived in fragments because it burned. The Voynich manuscript survived because it was locked away. The Sumerian hymns survived because a building collapsed.

Knowledge survives us, but usually only by accident.

The Mesopotamians knew something about music that we've been slow to appreciate: that the relationship between sound and mathematics is universal. The ratios that make a perfect fifth consonant exist in the physical properties of vibrating strings. They're not a cultural invention. A string that vibrates at twice the frequency of another produces an octave, no matter where you are on Earth or when you're listening.

The Mesopotamians discovered this 5,000 years ago. They pressed their discovery into clay. The clay survived. And 3,400 years later, we can still hear what they heard.

That's distribution. That's knowledge surviving us. A hymn to an apple goddess, written on clay, baked by fire, preserved by accident, decoded by scholars, and now readable by anyone with a computer. The goddess Nikkal has been dead for three millennia. Her song is still here.
